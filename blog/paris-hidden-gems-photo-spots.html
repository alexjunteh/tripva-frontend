15 Hidden Photo Spots in Paris You Haven't Seen Yet

May 22, 2026 8 min read By Tripva Editorial

In This Guide

  1. The 15 Spots
  2. How to Use These Spots

Paris is the most photographed city on earth, which means its iconic shots are also its most crowded and most replicated. The Trocadéro angle on the Eiffel Tower, the Sacré-Cœur steps at sunset, the Shakespeare & Company bookshop facade — beautiful, yes, but your Instagram already has them from fifty people you follow.

These 15 spots are different. Some require a 10-minute detour from a tourist circuit. Some require a specific season. A few require patience and the willingness to show up early. All of them produce photographs that look like yours and nobody else's.

Before you go
  • Golden hour in Paris: roughly 7am and 8–9pm in summer; 8am and 5pm in winter
  • Most hidden spots are busiest on weekends — go Tuesday through Thursday
  • A tripod is officially prohibited in some public spaces, but a mini GorillaPod is fine
  • Paris Museum Pass (€55, 2 days) covers several of the sites below

The 15 Spots

  • 1
    Rue Crémieux
    12th arrondissement · Near Gare de Lyon
    A short pedestrian street lined with pastel houses — bright yellow, turquoise, coral, rose — that look like they belong in Notting Hill or Valparaíso. The street is residential and genuinely quiet on weekday mornings. Every house is a different colour, making it impossible to take a bad photo from any angle down the lane.
    Best: weekday 8–10am Tip: cloudy days saturate the colours more than direct sun
  • 2
    Passage des Panoramas
    2nd arrondissement · Grands Boulevards
    Paris's oldest covered gallery (1799) has tiled floors, iron and glass ceilings, and a warm amber light from the shop fronts that makes every shot look like it was taken in another century. Stamp dealers, old theatre posters, and antique postcard shops line both sides. The light is best in the morning when sunlight filters through the skylights at an angle.
    Best: weekday morning, winter Tip: shoot toward the skylights for dramatic rays of light
  • 3
    Canal Saint-Martin Lock No. 2 (Écluse des Récollets)
    10th arrondissement · République
    The Canal Saint-Martin's iron footbridges and green water are well-known from Amélie. What tourists miss is the working canal lock just north of Hôpital Saint-Louis, where narrow barges rise and fall between the lock gates while locals fish on the banks. The reflections of the metal footbridge in the dark water create near-perfect symmetry.
    Best: early morning or overcast Tip: check barge schedules — a moving barge breaks the symmetry nicely
  • 4
    Bibliothèque de l'Assemblée Nationale (Salle des Quatre Colonnes)
    7th arrondissement · Palais Bourbon
    During European Heritage Days (Journées du Patrimoine, third weekend of September), the National Assembly opens its stunning neoclassical library to the public. The golden reading room with its columns and painted ceiling is among the most beautiful interiors in France — and virtually no one outside France knows it's accessible once a year. Free entry, guided tours run hourly.
    Once a year: 3rd weekend September Tip: queue by 9am — capacity is limited
  • 5
    Père Lachaise Cemetery — Division 89 Rooftop View
    20th arrondissement · Gambetta
    Père Lachaise's elevated eastern section (divisions 85–97) offers a surprising panorama over a sea of monuments, mausoleums, and tree canopies — with Haussman Paris visible in the gaps. In autumn the cemetery turns orange and gold. The grave of photographer Nadar and the memorial to the Paris Commune's last stand are nearby, adding historical weight to the frame.
    Best: autumn mornings, misty days Tip: visit on a weekday — weekends see Jim Morrison pilgrims
  • 6
    Musée des Arts et Métiers — Airplane Chapel
    3rd arrondissement · Arts et Métiers
    A 12th-century priory church repurposed as a museum gallery — and inside it hangs Clément Ader's 1897 Avion III steam-powered aircraft from the vaulted ceiling. Vintage calculating machines, Foucault's original pendulum, and early automata surround you. It's dark, atmospheric, and almost entirely unknown to non-Parisian tourists.
    Best: midday, wide lens Tip: shoot upward — the church's vaulted ceiling with the aircraft is the money shot
  • 7
    Villa de l'Ermitage
    20th arrondissement · Ménilmontant
    A hidden cobblestone lane in the 20th that feels like a village street from the 1900s — ivy-covered houses, working garden plots, and a complete absence of tourist traffic. It branches off the busy rue de Ménilmontant and you'd walk straight past without noticing the narrow entrance. Cats sleep on the window ledges. The whole lane photographs like a different century.
    Best: any morning Tip: also explore neighbouring Cité de l'Ermitage — a similar hidden lane nearby
  • 8
    Palais Royal Gardens Colonnade
    1st arrondissement · Palais Royal
    Tourists photograph Daniel Buren's striped columns in the courtyard. What they miss is the inner garden colonnade: long arcades of stone arches, perfectly symmetrical, warm late-afternoon light projecting column shadows across the flagstones. Shoot from the corner of the arcade looking down the length — the vanishing point is immaculate.
    Best: late afternoon sun, winter low light Tip: the arcades shelter small galleries and vintage coin shops worth browsing
  • 9
    Promenade Plantée (La Coulée Verte)
    12th arrondissement · Bastille to Vincennes
    The world's first elevated park, built on a disused 19th-century rail viaduct — the inspiration for New York's High Line. Roses, bamboo, and lavender beds run 4.5km above street level, past Haussmann apartment windows close enough to see the bookshelves inside. The viaduct arches below house design studios and artisan workshops (Les Viaducs des Arts).
    Best: May–June when roses bloom Tip: walk the full length east — it gets quieter and more secluded beyond the Gare de Lyon
  • 10
    Square du Vert-Galant at Dawn
    1st arrondissement · Île de la Cité
    The pointed tip of the Île de la Cité — the oldest part of Paris — sits just below the Pont Neuf. Before 8am, fishermen have the benches and the Seine's mist still hangs over the water. Both riverbanks, the Pont Neuf's arches, and Notre-Dame's towers are all visible from one position. It's been a beloved painters' spot since the 17th century.
    Best: dawn, misty autumn mornings Tip: access via the stairs descending from Pont Neuf; often locked until 7:30am
  • 11
    Notre-Dame North Rose Window (Interior)
    4th arrondissement · Île de la Cité
    Notre-Dame's nave reopened in late 2024 after the fire restoration. The north rose window — 13th century, 12.9 metres across — illuminates the transept with blue and violet light on winter mornings when the sun is low. The angle from the crossing, looking straight up at the vaulted ceiling through the restored scaffolding-free space, is unforgettable and finally accessible again.
    Best: winter mornings 9–11am, weekdays Tip: book a timed entry slot online — walk-up queues are 2+ hours
  • 12
    Montmartre Vineyard (Clos Montmartre)
    18th arrondissement · Montmartre
    Behind a locked iron gate on Rue des Saules sits a genuine 2,000-vine Paris vineyard. It's normally closed, but during the Fête des Vendanges Montmartre (October harvest festival) the vineyard opens and the streets fill with barrels, folk dancers in period costume, and the smell of crushed grapes. Even from outside the gate, the vines against the Montmartre stone walls make a striking frame.
    Best: October harvest festival Tip: pair with Musée de Montmartre directly next door for Renoir-era interiors
  • 13
    Sainte-Chapelle Upper Chapel
    1st arrondissement · Île de la Cité
    Most visitors skip Sainte-Chapelle in favour of Notre-Dame. This is a mistake. The 13th-century upper chapel has 1,113 biblical scenes in 15 stained glass windows — 15 metres tall, spanning almost the entire wall surface. On a sunny morning, the entire interior turns kaleidoscopic blue, red, and gold. There are almost no competing angles — every photo in here looks extraordinary.
    Best: sunny mornings 9–11am Tip: book combined ticket with the Conciergerie to skip the queue
  • 14
    Institut du Monde Arabe Terrace
    5th arrondissement · Latin Quarter
    The rooftop terrace of Jean Nouvel's 1987 masterpiece gives a panoramic view south over the Seine with Notre-Dame's scaffolding-free silhouette to the west. The building's south facade — 240 motorised metallic light-filtering panels based on Arabic geometric patterns — is also photogenic in its own right, especially in raking light that makes the patterns cast shadows.
    Best: golden hour, clear days Tip: museum admission required for terrace access; Thursday–Sunday open until 9:30pm
  • 15
    Bois de Vincennes — Lac Daumesnil at Dawn
    12th arrondissement · Eastern Paris
    The lake in the Bois de Vincennes holds two small islands connected by rustic footbridges, a neo-Byzantine Buddhist temple (open to visitors), and rowing boats for hire. On autumn mornings, ground mist drifts across the water and the yellow-leaved trees reflect perfectly. It's 20 minutes by Métro from the centre, and you'll often have the lakefront entirely to yourself before 9am.
    Best: autumn 7–9am, misty mornings Tip: take Métro Line 1 to Château de Vincennes and walk 10 minutes

How to Use These Spots

Don't try to hit all 15 in one trip. Group them by arrondissement and walk between them — the best Paris photography often happens in the streets between destinations, not at the spots themselves. Pick 4–5 per day and allow time to stay if the light is working.

The golden rule: arrive first, then decide. If a spot is already crowded by the time you get there, come back at dusk. Paris empties from 7pm in winter and 9pm in summer. The city at those edges of light is the city at its most extraordinary.
